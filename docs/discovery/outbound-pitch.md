## Core Principle: YOU Pull, WE Read

The discovery mechanism is built on one idea: **the user extracts their own data, using rights they already have. We never touch their accounts. They hand us a file, we find the patterns.**

This is not just a privacy feature -- it's the entire positioning:

- **GDPR gives every EU citizen the right to export their data.** We're the first product that makes that right useful.
- **We never ask for passwords, OAuth tokens, or API keys.** No account connections. No permissions screens. No "Allow access to..." dialogs.
- **The user stays in control at every step.** They choose what to export, what to upload, what to share. We're the analyst, not the collector.
- **This kills the #1 objection ("that sounds creepy") before it even starts.** We can't read your emails because we never had access to your email. You gave us a file. That's it.

5. **"Google, Apple, and Meta have your data. We help you use it for YOU."**
   *Provocative. Implies the power dynamic is broken and we fix it. Resonates with privacy-conscious Gen Z.*

## 2. "How It Works" Explanation (Landing Page -- 3 Sentences)

No jargon. No "data sources." No "algorithms." A normal person reading this on their phone should nod along.

> **You already have a goldmine of data about yourself** -- your Screen Time report, your calendar, your browser history. You just never knew what to do with it. **Upload any of it (or just answer 5 quick questions), and we'll show you the exact things worth automating** -- like "you check Zillow 8 times a day" or "you spend 3 hours every Monday copying numbers between apps." **Pick one, and we build it for you. Done in 30 minutes.**

### Why this works

- **Sentence 1** flips the script: the user isn't giving us data, they're *unlocking value* from data they already own. "Goldmine" creates curiosity. "You just never knew what to do with it" is the hook.
- **Sentence 2** gives the range (upload OR just answer questions) so nobody feels forced. The examples are hyper-specific and recognizable.
- **Sentence 3** is the payoff. "Pick one" = agency. "We build it for you" = no effort. "Done in 30 minutes" = concrete promise.

## 4. Objection Responses

### Objection: "That sounds creepy"

**What they're really saying:** "I don't want software watching everything I do on my computer."

**Response:**

> I get it -- but here's the thing: we literally can't watch you. We don't connect to your accounts. We don't install anything on your computer. We don't have your password for anything.
>
> What happens is: YOU go to your iPhone and screenshot your Screen Time. Or YOU export your Google Calendar. Or you just answer 5 questions about your week. Then you hand us that file and we analyze it. We never had access to anything -- you pulled it, you chose what to share, you uploaded it.
>
> It's like showing your doctor a blood test result. The doctor reads the report. The doctor didn't draw the blood or watch you at the lab.

**Why this works:**
- The doctor analogy is perfect for the self-export model. Everyone understands: the patient brings the test, the doctor reads it. Clear boundary.
- "We literally can't" is stronger than "we don't." It's a structural impossibility, not a policy choice.
- Lists the three options (screenshot, export, chat) to show the user is always the one acting.

---

### Objection: "I don't want you reading my emails"

**What they're really saying:** "I've been burned by data-hungry apps before."

**Response:**

> We can't read your emails. Not "we promise we won't" -- we can't. We never ask for your email login. We never connect to Gmail or Outlook. We have zero access to any of your accounts.
>
> What we CAN read: a screenshot of your Screen Time, a calendar export, or your browser history file -- but only if YOU download it and hand it to us. You choose what's in the file. You choose when to share. You can delete it from our servers afterward.
>
> We're a Finnish company under GDPR. If we mishandled your data, the Finnish Data Protection Authority would shut us down -- not fine us, shut us down. But honestly, the bigger protection is simpler: we never had access to your stuff in the first place.

**Why this works:**
- "Not 'we promise we won't' -- we can't" draws a hard line between policy and architecture. Architecture wins every time.
- The structural argument ("we never had access") is more persuasive than any privacy policy could ever be.
- GDPR enforcement is the backup, not the primary defense. The primary defense is that we literally don't have the keys.

---

### Objection: "I can just use ChatGPT for this"

**What they're really saying:** "Why do I need another AI thing?"

**Response:**

> You totally can paste your Screen Time data into ChatGPT. But you'd need to know what to ask, how to format it, and what to do with the answer. And you'd start over every single time.
>
> We do three things ChatGPT doesn't: (1) we tell you exactly how to export your data (step-by-step, with screenshots), (2) we analyze it against patterns from thousands of other people and give you specific, ranked suggestions, and (3) we actually BUILD the automation for you. ChatGPT gives you advice. We give you a working app.

---

### Objection: "What if I don't want to share any data at all?"

**What they're really saying:** "I want the benefit without any input."

**Response:**

> Just answer 5 questions about your week. No screenshots, no uploads, no data at all. We'll show you what's worth automating based on your answers alone.
>
> The data exports just make the suggestions more specific. But the 5-question version works great on its own -- most people start there and decide to share more after they see the first result.

---

### Objection: "How do I know you delete my data after?"

**What they're really saying:** "I've heard this promise before and been lied to."

**Response:**

> Two things. First: you can delete your uploads yourself, anytime, from your dashboard. It's not a request -- it's a button. The file disappears from our servers within 24 hours.
>
> Second: we're a Finnish company, which means GDPR is enforced by the Finnish Data Protection Authority. They don't issue gentle warnings -- they shut companies down. Our legal obligation is to delete your data within 30 days of your request, and our architecture is built to do it in 24 hours.
>
> But here's the real answer: the data you upload (a Screen Time screenshot, a calendar export) is a snapshot. It's not live. There's nothing to "keep watching" because we never connected to anything. Once you delete the upload, there's nothing left.

## 5. The Comparison Frame

### Primary analogy:

> **"It's like showing your accountant your bank statements. They find the waste you missed."**

You pull your own financial records. The accountant reads them and says "you're spending $400/month on subscriptions you don't use." You didn't give the accountant access to your bank -- you handed them a PDF.

We work the same way. You pull your Screen Time, your calendar, your browser history. We read it and say "you're wasting 6 hours a week on things a bot could handle." You didn't give us access to your phone -- you handed us a screenshot.

**Why the accountant works as the reference:**
- Everyone understands the accountant-client relationship. The client brings the documents. The accountant reads them.
- It's a trusted professional relationship. Accountants see sensitive data. Nobody thinks it's "creepy" because the boundaries are clear.
- The value proposition is identical: "I know what these numbers mean better than you do. Let me find the waste."
- It naturally positions us as experts, not surveillance tools.

### Backup analogies:

> **"It's like a nutritionist reading your food diary, not watching you eat."**
> You write down what you ate. They tell you what to change. They never stood in your kitchen.

*Best for:* health-conscious audiences. Very clear on the "you bring it, we read it" boundary.

> **"It's like Shazam, but for wasted time."**
> Shazam listens to what's playing around you and tells you the song name. We look at your Screen Time and tell you what to automate. The magic is identifying what you couldn't see yourself.

*Best for:* Gen Z audiences. Shazam is universally known. Captures the "discovery" magic. Less precise on the self-export model but catchier.

> **"Your data is a map of your life. Google reads it for ads. We read it for YOU."**
> Same data, opposite purpose. They profit from knowing you. We help you profit from knowing yourself.

*Best for:* privacy-conscious, anti-Big-Tech audiences. Provocative. Positions us as the ethical alternative to what already happens.

## 6. The Self-Export Discovery System

### The Principle

Every major platform is legally required to let users export their data (GDPR Article 20, CCPA, etc.). This data is rich, structured, and sitting unused on every user's device. We don't need to build data collection infrastructure -- we just need to:

1. **Tell them exactly how to export** (step-by-step tutorials with screenshots)
2. **Accept the file they upload** (drag-and-drop, no formatting required)
3. **Analyze it with an LLM** (pattern matching against our use-case library)
4. **Show them what to automate** (ranked suggestions with time-saved estimates)

**We never touch their accounts. We're the analyst, not the collector.**

### Source #1: SCREEN TIME SCREENSHOT (The Hero Move)

**How the user gets it:**
- iPhone: Settings > Screen Time > See All App & Website Activity > screenshot
- Android: Settings > Digital Wellbeing > Dashboard > screenshot

**Step-by-step tutorial we provide (3 steps, with device-specific screenshots):**
1. Open Settings on your phone
2. Tap Screen Time (iPhone) or Digital Wellbeing (Android)
3. Screenshot the summary screen and upload it here

**What one screenshot gives us:**
- Top 10-20 apps by daily usage (hours + minutes)
- Category breakdown (Social, Productivity, Entertainment, etc.)
- Pickups per day (how often they reach for their phone)
- Notifications per day by app
- Daily/weekly usage trends

**What we infer and suggest:**
| Pattern We See | What We Say | Automation We Suggest |
|---------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Gmail 47min + Sheets 23min | "You spend over an hour a day between email and spreadsheets. Are you copying data between them?" | Email-to-spreadsheet auto-import |
| Zillow/apartments.fi checked 8x/day | "You're checking listings 8 times a day. Want alerts instead of manual refreshing?" | New listing push notification |
| Instagram 2hr + TikTok 1.5hr | "3.5 hours of social media. What if your posts went out automatically and you just checked replies?" | Cross-platform auto-poster |
| Slack 3hr but low "Productivity" category | "You're in Slack more than any work tool. What are you coordinating that a bot could handle?" | Standup/status auto-responder |
| 85 pickups/day | "85 pickups a day is 2+ hours of context switching. Let's figure out which notifications actually matter." | Notification filter/digest |

**Why this is the unfair hack:**
- Zero install. Zero permissions. Zero API. Zero privacy risk.
- Apple/Google already collected this for free. The user already has it.
- A screenshot is the most natural sharing gesture for Gen Z.
- The LLM does all the heavy lifting -- just send the image with a prompt. No OCR pipeline, no parsing code.
- **Build time: a file upload endpoint + one LLM vision call.** We could ship this in a day.

**How to pitch it:**
> "Take a screenshot of your Screen Time and upload it. We'll show you exactly where your time goes and what to automate. Takes 10 seconds. We never touch your phone."

### Source #2: THE 5-QUESTION CHAT (Zero-Data Entry Point)

**The 5 questions:**

1. **"What's the most annoying part of your Monday?"**
   *(Opens with emotion. People vent. The vent contains the use case.)*

2. **"What do you copy-paste more than once a week?"**
   *(Copy-paste = a human being middleware. Direct automation signal.)*

3. **"What website or app do you check more than 3 times a day?"**
   *(Polling behavior = monitoring opportunity.)*

4. **"What's something you keep meaning to organize but never do?"**
   *(The "I'll do it later" pile -- downloads, receipts, notes, photos.)*

5. **"If you had a perfect assistant for one hour a day, what would they do?"**
   *(Dream question. Reveals the highest-value automation they'd pay for.)*

**Why this is the entry point:**
- Zero data, zero trust, zero friction.
- Every question maps to a research synthesis pattern (polling->push, manual tracking, decision fatigue, scattered data).
- Feels like a personality quiz, not an interrogation. Gen Z loves quizzes.
- **Can BE the landing page.** No signup gate. Answer 5 questions, get results, THEN we ask for the email.

### Source #3: BROWSER HISTORY EXPORT

**How the user gets it:**
- Chrome: `chrome://history` > three-dot menu > Export (or use Chrome Takeout for full JSON)
- Firefox: `about:support` > Profile Directory > `places.sqlite`
- Safari: Bookmarks > Show All History > (limited export, or `~/Library/Safari/History.db`)

**Step-by-step tutorial we provide:**
1. Open Chrome and type `chrome://history` in the address bar
2. Click the three dots in the top right, then "Export browsing data"
3. Upload the file here

**What we learn:**
- Most-visited sites by frequency and recency
- Polling behavior (same URL 5+ times/day = obsessive checking)
- Research spirals (20 tabs on one topic = decision fatigue)
- Time-of-day patterns (when they work, when they shop, when they browse)
- The sites they actually USE vs. the ones they just visit

---

### Source #4: GOOGLE TAKEOUT

**How the user gets it:**
- Go to `takeout.google.com`
- Select which data to include (we recommend: Calendar, Chrome, YouTube, Search, Location)
- Click "Create export"
- Wait for email notification (1 hour to 2 days)
- Download ZIP and upload to us

**Step-by-step tutorial we provide:**
1. Go to takeout.google.com and sign in
2. Click "Deselect all", then check only: Calendar, Chrome, YouTube, My Activity
3. Click "Next step" then "Create export"
4. Wait for the email (usually 1-2 hours), download the ZIP
5. Upload the ZIP file here -- we only read the parts you selected

**What we learn from each sub-export:**

| Data | What It Reveals |
|------|----------------|
| **Calendar** | Meeting load, recurring time sinks, scheduling patterns, free time gaps |
| **Chrome history** | Browsing patterns, polling sites, research topics, time-of-day habits |
| **YouTube history** | What they're learning vs. procrastinating on, content consumption patterns |
| **Search history** | What they research, worry about, shop for, troubleshoot repeatedly |
| **My Activity** | Cross-app usage timeline, which Google services they bounce between |

**Why this is the deep dive:**
- Richest single export available. Google has more data about most people than any other company.
- GDPR Article 20 guarantees the right to this data. We're just helping users exercise it.
- One upload, multiple insight streams.

**Why it's not the entry point:**
- Takes 1-24 hours for Google to prepare the export. The user has to come back later.
- The ZIP is big and intimidating. Users worry about what's in it.
- Higher trust barrier than a screenshot.

**How to pitch the upgrade (after screenshot results):**

> "Your Screen Time showed us the surface. Your Google Takeout would show us the full picture -- what you search for, what meetings eat your week, what you watch when you should be working. Want to go deeper? Here's how to export it in 3 clicks."

### Source #5-9: ADDITIONAL EXPORTS (Future Paths)

These are lower-priority but available for users who want to go deep:

**Meta Data Download:** Reveals ad interest categories (what Facebook thinks you care about), messaging frequency patterns, login time distribution. The ad interests alone are a goldmine: "Facebook thinks you care about 'real estate investment' and 'meal prep' -- want automations for those?"

**Calendar .ics Export:** Lighter than full Takeout. Just the calendar events. Good for meeting-heavy professionals.

**Spotify/YouTube History:** Procrastination signal detection. Learning interest mapping. "You watched 14 hours of Excel tutorials last month -- want an automation instead?"

**Bank Statements:** Subscription audit. "You're paying for 6 services that overlap. Here's which ones to cancel and what to replace them with." (High sensitivity -- probably Phase 2.)

This is the meta-story that ties everything together for marketing, PR, and positioning.

### The Narrative

> Big Tech companies collect your data to sell ads. You have the legal right to get that data back. But when you do, it arrives as a useless ZIP file full of JSON nobody can read.
>
> We're the first product that makes your own data useful FOR YOU.
>
> Upload your Screen Time screenshot, your Google Takeout, your browser history -- any data export you already have the right to pull. We read it and show you exactly where your time goes, what you should automate, and then we build the automation for you.
>
> Your data was always a goldmine. Google just kept the map.

### Why this narrative is powerful

1. **It positions us as the user's ally against Big Tech.** We're not another company asking for data -- we're the company that helps you USE your data.
2. **GDPR becomes a feature, not a compliance burden.** We're the first product built on data portability rights.

4. **It kills privacy objections structurally.** We can't be creepy because we never had access to anything. The user pulled their own data and chose to share it.
5. **It creates a new category.** Not "activity monitoring." Not "data analytics." This is "personal data intelligence" -- turning your own data into action.

### PR / Content angles

- "What your Screen Time report actually means (and what to do about it)"
- "I exported my Google Takeout. Here's what Google knows about me."

Each of these is a standalone content piece that drives organic traffic AND demonstrates the product.
