# Discovery Mechanism: Positioning Strategy

**Date:** 2026-03-30
**Purpose:** Define how Meldar talks about its discovery capability externally -- naming, metaphor, differentiation, narrative arc, and win themes for the landing page.

---

## The Strategic Problem

Meldar's discovery mechanism is the only thing no competitor has. Lovable builds apps but makes you think of what to build. Zapier connects apps but makes you choose the workflow. MuleRun and Minro observe behavior but don't build anything with the insights. Discovery is the moat.

But "discovery" is internal vocabulary. It sounds like an analytics feature. Non-technical people hear "discovery" and think Netflix recommendations or Spotify Discover Weekly -- passive, low-stakes, algorithmic. What Meldar does is higher-stakes and more personal: it watches how you actually spend your time, finds the waste you can't see, and tells you exactly what to fix.

The external name must do three things:

1. **Feel personal** -- this is about YOUR life, not "workflows"
2. **Imply a reveal** -- something you didn't know about yourself
3. **Create urgency** -- the waste is happening right now, every day

## 1. What Do We Call It?

### Recommendation: **Time X-Ray**

| Candidate | Pros | Cons | Verdict |
|-----------|------|------|---------|
| Time Audit | Familiar, concrete, business-validated | Sounds like accounting. Passive. Implies a one-time report, not an ongoing capability | GOOD FOR THE FREE TIER OFFER ("Get your free Time X-Ray") -- see below |
| Life Scan | Personal, medical-ish urgency | Creepy. Surveillance connotation. "Life" is too broad -- sounds like it reads your diary | NO |
| Pattern Finder | Accurate | Generic, sounds like a data science tool | NO |
| Habit Mirror | Evocative | Implies judgment. "Mirror" = vanity | NO |
| Routine Report | Clear | Boring. "Report" = homework | NO |
| Time X-Ray | Medical precision. Implies seeing what's hidden. Concrete visual metaphor. "X-Ray" = fast, specific, revealing | Slightly clinical | **YES** |
| Time Map | Spatial, explorable, less clinical | Weaker urgency than X-Ray | STRONG SECOND |
| Blind Spot Finder | High urgency, implies danger | Slightly negative/accusatory | THIRD |

**"Time X-Ray" wins because:**

- X-rays reveal what you can't see with the naked eye. That's exactly what this does -- you can't see your own time waste because you're inside it.
- X-rays are fast. You don't sit in an X-ray machine for weeks. You go in, you get the image, you see the problem. This maps to the product experience.
- X-rays are done by professionals, not by patients. The user doesn't have to do the analysis. Meldar does.
- The word "time" anchors it to what people actually care about: their hours, not their "workflows."
- X-rays are not surveillance. Nobody objects to getting an X-ray. The metaphor implies care, not watching.

### Usage in copy

| Context | Phrasing |
|---------|----------|
| Feature name on landing page | "Your Time X-Ray" |
| CTA for free tier | "Get your free Time X-Ray" |
| Explaining what it does | "Meldar's Time X-Ray shows you exactly where your hours go -- and which ones you can get back" |
| In the dashboard | "Your X-Ray" (shortened, once the user is inside the product) |
| Section header | "See what you're missing" |

### Alternate naming for different contexts

- **Formal/investor context:** "Behavioral time analysis" -- for pitch decks where "X-Ray" is too casual
- **Casual/social context:** "Your time X-Ray" or just "the X-Ray" -- for tweets, short-form content
- **The free offer (early-adopter hook):** "Personal Time Audit" -- for the Founding 50 package, the human-delivered version. "Audit" works here because someone is personally doing it for you, like a financial auditor would. Once productized, the feature becomes "Time X-Ray."

## 2. What Metaphor Works?

### Recommendation: The Doctor's Checkup

**Not** "a personal assistant who watches your day." That's surveillance. Non-technical users who are already anxious about AI tools don't want something watching them. The assistant metaphor also implies servitude and ongoing presence, which is creepy for a first interaction.

**The right metaphor is medical:**

> "It's like getting an annual checkup -- but for how you spend your time. The doctor doesn't just ask 'how do you feel?' They run tests. They find things you didn't notice. They say 'this number is too high, and here's what to do about it.' That's what Meldar does for your daily routine."

**Why the medical metaphor works for this audience:**

| Medical concept | Meldar equivalent | Why it resonates |
|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| The doctor finds things before they become serious | Meldar finds time waste before you burn out | Prevention framing -- act early |
| You can't diagnose yourself | You can't see your own patterns | Justifies why you need the tool |
| The doctor gives a specific prescription | Meldar gives a specific automation to build | Expert authority + specific action |
| You go willingly for a checkup | You opt into the scan voluntarily | Eliminates surveillance concern |
| The checkup is periodic | The Time X-Ray updates regularly | Ongoing value, not one-time |
| The doctor sees your body, not your thoughts | Meldar sees your app usage, not your content | Privacy boundary is intuitive |

**Supporting metaphors for different contexts:**

| Context | Metaphor |
|---------|----------|
| Explaining to skeptics | "It's like a fitness tracker for your productivity -- it counts the steps, you decide what to do about it" |
| Explaining the reveal moment | "It's like checking your screen time report, except instead of just feeling bad about it, you get a button that fixes it" |
| Explaining to technical users | "Think of it as profiling for your daily life -- it identifies the hot paths and suggests optimizations" |

## 4. What's the Story Arc?

### Act 1: Before Meldar -- The Invisible Bleed

The person doesn't know they have a problem. Or they know vaguely ("I'm always busy") but can't point to what's eating their time. They've tried:

- Checking their screen time report (felt bad, did nothing)
- Asking ChatGPT for productivity tips (got generic advice)
- Trying Zapier (hit a wall at "select a trigger")
- Watching a YouTube tutorial on AI automation (it broke the next day)

**Emotional state:** Frustrated, vaguely guilty, overwhelmed. Suspects AI could help but has failed every time they tried. Starting to believe "maybe AI isn't for people like me."

**Key insight:** The waste is invisible. You don't notice you spend 40 minutes choosing what to cook because you've always done it. You don't notice you check the apartment listings site 6 times a day because it's a reflex. The problem isn't that you're lazy -- it's that you're inside the pattern and can't see it.

### Act 2: The Moment of Truth -- The X-Ray Reveals

Meldar runs. The Time X-Ray comes back. The person sees, for the first time, a clear picture:

> "You spent 6.2 hours this week on tasks that could be automated. Here are the top 3."

This is the emotional peak. It's not "Meldar is cool technology." It's **"I had no idea."** The reveal is personal, specific, and slightly uncomfortable -- like stepping on a scale after the holidays.

**Key insight:** The number must be specific and personal. Not "most people waste 5 hours." THIS person, THIS week, THESE tasks. Generic doesn't create the reaction. Specific does.

**Emotional state:** Surprise, relief ("it's not my fault"), urgency ("I want those hours back"), curiosity ("what else is it finding?").

### Act 3: After Meldar -- The Reclaimed Life

The person now has automations running. The meal planner decides dinner. The price watcher pings when the flight drops. The expense tracker categorizes receipts without being asked. The hours are back.

But the real shift isn't the hours. It's the mental load. They're not carrying those tasks in their head anymore. The cognitive background noise -- "I should check that site," "I need to update that spreadsheet," "what's for dinner" -- is gone.

**Key insight:** Automation doesn't just save time. It saves attention. The after state isn't "I have 5 extra hours." It's "I stopped thinking about things that don't matter."

**Emotional state:** Calm, competent, slightly evangelical ("you have to try this").

### The Arc in One Paragraph (for copy reference)

> You're losing hours every week to tasks you don't even notice anymore. Checking, copying, tracking, deciding -- it adds up, and you're too close to see it. Meldar's Time X-Ray shows you exactly where your time goes. Not a vague screen-time report -- a specific list of what's eating your week and what to do about each one. The people who see their X-Ray for the first time always say the same thing: "I had no idea." Then they fix it. And they never go back.

## 6. The Unfair Positioning Advantage: "Your Data. Your Export. Our Analysis."

### Founder Directive (v2)

Users pull their OWN data from tools they already use -- GDPR data exports (Google Takeout, Meta data download, Apple privacy report), Screen Time screenshots, browser history exports. The user does the extraction themselves using rights they already have. We tell them exactly how (step-by-step tutorial). They hand us a file. We find the patterns. We never touch their accounts.

### Why This Is a Better Story Than OAuth

The previous version of this section recommended "Sign in with Google" as the primary path. The founder's update is strategically superior for three reasons:

**1. Trust is the product, not the obstacle.**

With OAuth, trust is friction we need to overcome ("yes, we'll access your Google account, but we only read metadata..."). With self-export, trust is the selling point. We are the ONLY product that never asks for your password, never connects to your accounts, never has standing access to anything. You hand us a file. We read it. We delete it. That's a story people WANT to tell their friends.

**2. It turns a legal right into a product feature.**

GDPR Article 20 gives every EU citizen the right to export their data in a portable format. Google Takeout, Meta data download, Apple privacy report -- these all exist because of the law. But nobody has ever given users a REASON to use that right. Meldar does. "You have a legal right to your data. Use it. We'll show you what it means." This is positioning no competitor can copy because it requires building the analysis layer on top of messy, varied export formats.

**3. It's platform-agnostic from Day 1.**

OAuth locks you into specific providers (Google ecosystem). Self-export works with anything that has an export button: Google, Apple, Meta, Spotify, banking apps, fitness trackers. The positioning becomes "bring us any data about your life" -- not "connect your Google account."

### The New Positioning Narrative: "Reclaim Your Data"

The emotional core shifts from "we read what Google has" to something more powerful: **"This is YOUR data. You're the only person who's never seen it."**

Google has it. Meta has it. Apple has it. They use it to sell you ads, recommend content, keep you scrolling. They never show it to YOU in a way that helps YOU. They make money from your patterns. You get nothing.

Meldar is the first product that says: **"Download your data. Hand it to us. We'll tell you what they've been seeing all along -- and then we'll fix it."**

This is a David-vs-Goliath narrative. The big platforms hoard your data. GDPR gives you the right to take it back. Meldar gives you a reason to.

### The Step-by-Step Tutorial as Product

Here's the insight most competitors miss: **the tutorial IS a product.**

Nobody has ever told a non-technical person: "You have a legal right to download all your Google data. Here's how, in 6 screenshots." That tutorial -- clear, visual, hand-holding -- is valuable on its own. It could go viral as a standalone piece of content. "I just found out I can download everything Google knows about me. Here's how."

The tutorial serves three functions:

1. **Content marketing.** "How to download your Google data" is a searchable, shareable topic. The tutorial drives organic traffic.
2. **Trust building.** Walking someone through THEIR OWN data export -- before asking them for anything -- is the ultimate reciprocity play. You gave them knowledge. Now they trust you with the file.
3. **Funnel entry.** The tutorial ends with "Now upload it here and see what it means." The user has invested 5 minutes and has a file on their desktop. Sunk cost + curiosity = high conversion.

**Copy for the tutorial CTA:**
- "Did you know you can download everything Google knows about you? Here's how (takes 4 minutes)."
- "Your Google data export is sitting right there. You just never had a reason to look at it. Until now."

The second version has two shareable hooks:
1. "You can download everything Google knows about you" (news to most people -- this goes viral on its own)
2. "An AI analyzed it and found I waste 6 hours" (the Time X-Ray result)

People share the self-export version because it contains a discovery ("I can download my data??") PLUS a reveal ("and here's what it said"). The OAuth version only has the reveal. Two hooks are better than one.

> "Meldar doesn't watch you. You show Meldar."

This six-word line should be the trust anchor across every piece of communication. It inverts the surveillance narrative that haunts every AI product. It puts the user in the driver's seat. And it's true.

These are internal or techie terms that should never appear on the landing page or in external communications:

| Internal term | External replacement |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Discovery engine | Time X-Ray |
| Activity pattern analysis | "We watch where your time goes" |
| Hook-based data aggregation | "We see which apps you use and how often" |
| Automation suggestions | "Your personal fix list" or "Recommendations" |
| Behavioral time analysis | "Your Time X-Ray" |
| Process mining | Never use. Enterprise term. |
| Workflow discovery | "Finding what's worth automating" |
| Agent orchestration | Never use. Nobody outside AI Twitter knows what this means. |

## Appendix: Competitive Positioning One-Liner

For social media, pitch decks, and quick comparisons:

> **Lovable:** "Tell us what to build."
> **Zapier:** "Connect these two apps."
> **Meldar:** "Let us show you what's eating your week -- then fix it."

The three-way comparison should appear in some form on every pitch deck, every social post, and every competitive conversation. It's the clearest way to communicate the gap.
