# Discovery Messaging Brainstorm

## 1. Taglines / Motos (10)

1. **"Your phone knows you better than you do. Time to read the receipts."**
   Your own data, turned against your own blind spots. "Receipts" is internet-native — it means proof.

2. **"6 hours a week. Gone. You just can't see it yet."**
   Leads with a specific, uncomfortable number. Creates the itch to find out YOUR number.

3. **"Google's been watching you for a decade. You've never watched back."**
   Flips the surveillance narrative. You're not the product — you're reclaiming the power.

4. **"You didn't waste 6 hours on purpose. That's the problem."**
   Non-judgmental but urgent. The waste is invisible, not intentional.

5. **"Spotify Wrapped, but for your wasted time."**
   Instantly understood by anyone under 30. Fun format, uncomfortable truth.

6. **"Your screen time report just felt bad. This one actually fixes things."**
   Directly positions against the feature everyone already knows and ignores.

7. **"You have 168 hours a week. Something's eating the ones you can't account for."**
   Math + mystery. Makes you want to know the answer.

8. **"Download your Google data. Find out what they already knew."**
   Two sentences, one punch. Activates the "wait, I can DO that?" moment.

9. **"The app that tells you what app to build."**
   Meta and punchy. Differentiates from every AI builder tool in six words.

10. **"Stop guessing what to automate. Your data already knows."**
    Speaks directly to the blank-prompt problem. Your data has the answers — you just need someone to read it.

## 2. Discovery Feature Names

The principle: every feature name should sound like something you'd tell a friend about, not something you'd find in a settings menu.

| What it actually does | Boring name (don't use) | Proposed name | Why it works |
|---|---|---|---|
| Analyzing screen time | Screen Time Analysis | **Phone Reality Check** | "Reality check" implies you've been lying to yourself. Casual, slightly confrontational, shareable. |
| Analyzing notifications | Notification Analysis | **The Ping Audit** | "Ping" is how Gen Z talks about notifications. "Audit" gives it weight without being corporate. |
| Analyzing subscriptions | Subscription Review | **The Leak Finder** | Money "leaking" from your account. Implies something broken that needs fixing. Urgency without drama. |
| Analyzing email/inbox | Email Analysis | **Inbox Autopsy** | You know those emails are dead on arrival. Dark humor, memorable, implies you'll finally deal with the corpse. |
| Analyzing calendar | Calendar Analysis | **The Ghost Hour Hunt** | "Ghost hours" = time that disappears into meetings you didn't need. Hunting them down sounds fun. |
| The combined full life audit | Full Analysis | **The Full X-Ray** | Extends the core "Time X-Ray" metaphor. Medical precision, comprehensive, nothing hidden. |

### Alternative naming system: "Your ___ Report"

If the brand wants consistency over personality, every feature could follow the pattern: **"Your [noun] Report"** — Your Phone Report, Your Inbox Report, Your Money Report, Your Calendar Report, Your Full Report. Clean, predictable, personal (the "Your" does the work).

The fun names are better for marketing and virality. The "Your ___ Report" system is better for the product UI once someone is inside the app. Use both: fun names in ads and social, clean names inside the dashboard.

## 3. The Discovery Story

### Transition 1: "I don't think I waste time" --> "Wait, maybe I do"

**The trigger:** A number. Not advice, not a lecture — a specific, personal, slightly embarrassing number.

> "You searched 'what should I eat for dinner' 23 times this month."

The user hasn't admitted they have a problem. They haven't asked for help. They just saw a fact about themselves they can't unsee. This is the design pattern of every effective health app — the scale, the step counter, the cholesterol number. You don't ARGUE with a number. You just look at it and go "oh."

**Copy that drives this transition:**

- Landing page hero / subheadline: "See where your time actually goes."
- CTA: "Get your Time X-Ray — free, 30 seconds."
- Social proof: "The average user finds 6.2 hours of invisible waste per week."
- Testimonial format: "I thought I was fine. Then I saw the number." (future)

**What NOT to say here:** Don't lead with "you're wasting time." People get defensive. Lead with curiosity: "Want to see your number?" The accusation comes from their own data, not from us.

### Transition 2: "Wait, maybe I do" --> "Holy shit"

**The trigger:** The gap between what they THOUGHT and what the data SHOWS.

This is the Spotify Wrapped moment. You thought you listened to indie music. Turns out your top genre is K-pop and you played that one Taylor Swift song 347 times. The surprise is the product.

For Meldar, the surprise is: "You think your biggest time-waster is Instagram. But actually, it's the 40 minutes every day you spend deciding what to cook, checking three recipe sites, comparing ingredients, and then ordering takeout anyway."

> "Your top time leak isn't what you think."

The X-Ray reveals patterns that span apps. Screen Time tells you "3 hours on Safari." Meldar tells you "47 minutes of that Safari time is you searching for apartments you never apply to." The specificity is what creates the "holy shit."

**Copy that drives this transition:**

- X-Ray results headline: "Here's what we found."
- Key stat treatment: Big number, bold, impossible to miss. "4.7 hours/week on meal decisions."
- The surprise line: "You thought it was [self-reported app]. It's actually [discovered pattern]."
- Share CTA: "Think your friends know their number? Send them this."

### Transition 3: "Holy shit" --> "Fix this for me"

**The trigger:** A button that says "Fix this" right next to the number.

The emotional window between "holy shit" and "I should do something" is about 8 seconds. If you make someone feel something and then ask them to navigate to a different page, open a form, describe what they want — you've lost them. The fix has to be ONE CLICK from the reveal.

> See the number. See the fix. Click.

The fix isn't "here are 10 tips for better time management." The fix is: "We'll build you a meal planner that already knows what you like. Want it?" Yes/No. Done.

**Copy that drives this transition:**

- Next to each finding: "Fix this" button (primary) / "Not now" (ghost)
- Fix description: One sentence. "A meal planner that learns what you like and suggests dinner every day at 5pm."
- Reassurance: "Takes 2 minutes to set up. Works on autopilot after that."
- Urgency (gentle): "Every week without this = another 4.7 hours gone."

## 4. Competitive Positioning

### vs. Apple Screen Time

**What they do:** Show you raw numbers. Hours per app. Pickups per day. A bar chart that makes you feel vaguely guilty every Sunday.

**What they don't do:** Tell you WHAT the time is spent on within apps. Tell you WHY you keep going back. Tell you what to DO about it. Offer any fix beyond "set a limit" (which everyone ignores).

**The line:** *"Screen Time tells you how long. Meldar tells you what to do about it."*

**The deeper argument:** Screen Time is a mirror — it shows you facts, then walks away. Meldar is a doctor — it shows you facts, diagnoses the pattern, and writes the prescription. Same data, completely different outcome.

### vs. RescueTime

**What they do:** Track time automatically across desktop apps. Categorize by "productive" vs "distracting." Weekly reports.

**What they don't do:** Act on the data. Build anything. Understand WHY you spent time on something (context matters — 2 hours in Figma could be work or procrastination). Work on mobile. Feel modern.

**The line:** *"RescueTime watches. Meldar watches AND fixes."*

**The deeper argument:** RescueTime is a decade-old tool built for knowledge workers who want dashboards. Meldar is for people who don't want another dashboard — they want the problem to go away. The output of RescueTime is "you spent 3 hours on email." The output of Meldar is "here's an email triage tool that cuts that to 45 minutes."

### vs. ChatGPT (generic AI advice)

**What they do:** Give great advice when you ask the right question. "How can I be more productive?" gets you a 10-bullet list that sounds smart and changes nothing.

**What they don't do:** Know anything about YOUR life. See your actual data. Remember that you asked the same question last month and did nothing. Build a persistent tool that runs without you.

**The line:** *"ChatGPT gives advice. Meldar gives proof — and then fixes what the proof shows."*

**The deeper argument:** The blank prompt is the problem. ChatGPT requires you to KNOW what to ask. But the whole point is that you don't know. You don't know you waste 40 minutes on dinner decisions because you've never counted. You can't ask ChatGPT to fix a problem you can't see. Meldar sees it for you, using your own data, and then builds the fix. No prompting required.

### The positioning matrix (for quick reference)

| | Sees your data | Finds patterns | Tells you what to do | Builds the fix | Never touches your accounts |
|---|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Apple Screen Time** | Yes | No | No | No | N/A (it IS the device) |
| **RescueTime** | Yes | Sort of | No | No | No (installs agent) |
| **ChatGPT** | No | No | Generic yes | No | N/A (no data access) |
| **Meldar** | Yes | Yes | Yes, specific | Yes | Yes |

## 5. The One-Liner (Hero)

The hero one-liner needs to: stop the scroll, be under 10 words, imply a reveal, and speak to someone who doesn't know they have a problem.

### Top candidates

1. **"See where your time actually goes."**
   Currently live on the site. Clean, clear, implies a gap between perception and reality. Strong.

2. **"Your week has a leak. We'll find it."**
   More attitude. "Leak" implies something fixable, not a character flaw. Under 10 words.

3. **"Find your invisible hours."**
   Six words. "Invisible" does the heavy lifting — it implies the hours exist but you can't see them. Mystery + promise.

4. **"What's eating your week?"**
   A question. Casual, slightly ominous. Feels like something you'd text a friend. Five words.

5. **"You're losing hours you don't know about."**
   Direct address, specific claim, creates curiosity. Nine words.

### Recommendation

**"What's eating your week?"** is the strongest scroll-stopper. Five words. Feels personal. Implies something is wrong that you haven't noticed. Creates an itch to find out the answer. And it naturally leads to the CTA: "Get your Time X-Ray."

Runner-up: **"Find your invisible hours."** — more poetic, slightly less urgent, but very clean.

The current live version — "See where your time actually goes" — is solid and working. It's clear and honest. The question format of "What's eating your week?" adds more tension and personality, which may test better with Gen Z. Worth A/B testing.
