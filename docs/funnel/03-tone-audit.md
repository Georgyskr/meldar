# Tone Audit: Landing Page Copy

**Audience:** Non-technical people (18-28) who are scared of AI and have failed before with AI tools.
**Target voice:** Confident but warm, like a friend who's brilliant at this. Direct. Personal. Zero jargon.

---

## Overall Assessment

The copy is **significantly better than most AI landing pages**. It reads human, avoids the worst startup cliches, and clearly speaks to someone who has been burned. The ProblemSection is particularly strong -- it reads like someone venting to a friend, which is exactly right.

That said, there are moments where the voice slips into "startup pitch mode," a handful of sentences that are too polished or too clever for a scared 20-year-old, and a few spots where the warmth drops out. Below is every issue, section by section.

---

## HeroSection

### Issue 1: Tagline feels like a tech brand slogan
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current:**
> Your AI. Your app. Nobody else's.

**Problem:** This reads like an Apple tagline. It's slick, but it's cold. A 20-year-old who just failed at setting up ChatGPT doesn't care about ownership yet -- they care about not feeling stupid again.

**Rewrite:**
> Finally. AI that actually works for you.

---

### Issue 2: "Let it figure out you" is clever but slightly creepy
**Type:** Oversells / tone mismatch

**Current:**
> Stop trying to figure out AI. Let it figure out you.

**Problem:** The reversal is clever copywriting, but "let it figure out you" sounds like surveillance to someone already nervous about AI. For a privacy-scared Gen Z audience, this triggers the wrong emotion.

**Rewrite:**
> Stop trying to figure out AI. Tell it what you need instead.

Or:
> You shouldn't have to learn AI. It should learn what you need.

---

### Issue 3: "No coding. No confusion." is slightly corporate-cadenced
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current:**
> Tell Meldar what annoys you. It builds a personal app that fixes it. 30 minutes. No coding. No confusion.

**Problem:** The first two sentences are great -- warm, direct, personal. "30 minutes. No coding. No confusion." switches into bullet-point marketing mode. The rhythm breaks.

**Rewrite:**
> Tell Meldar what annoys you. It builds a personal app that fixes it. Takes about 30 minutes. You don't write a single line of code.

---

## ProblemSection

### Issue 4: "configure" is borderline jargon
**Type:** Jargon

**Current:**
> ...enter codes into a black screen, and somehow "configure" something.

**Problem:** The scare quotes help, but the word itself might fly over the heads of truly non-technical people. The rest of this paragraph is brilliantly relatable, so this word sticks out.

**Rewrite:**
> ...enter codes into a black screen, and somehow "set up" something that made no sense.

---

### Issue 5: Section heading could hit harder
**Type:** Missed emotional opportunity

**Current:**
> Sound familiar?

**Problem:** It's fine, but it's also the heading on every "problem" section on every SaaS landing page since 2018. For an audience that has been burned, something more specific and empathetic would land better.

**Rewrite:**
> Been here before?

Or:
> Yeah. We've been there too.

---

### Issue 6: Testimonial attribution is too vague
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company / trust issue

**Current:**
> "I've tried every AI tool out there. Meldar is the first one that didn't make me feel stupid." -- Early beta user

**Problem:** "Early beta user" is startup language. It signals "we made this up" to a skeptical audience. Either give a name and context (even a first name and age) or frame it differently.

**Rewrite attribution:**
> -- Sara, 24, freelance designer

Or if it's not a real quote yet, remove it entirely and replace with a more specific pain statement.

---

## SkillCardsSection

### Issue 7: Section heading tone is slightly too polished
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current:**
> Things people build in their first week

**Problem:** "Their first week" implies an onboarding period -- it sounds like a SaaS dashboard, not a friend showing you something cool. The audience hasn't committed to a "week" of anything yet.

**Rewrite:**
> Ideas to steal (people actually built these)

Or:
> Real stuff people made with Meldar

---

### Issue 8: "Each takes minutes to set up" feels like ad copy
**Type:** Oversells

**Current:**
> Real problems. Personal apps. Each takes minutes to set up.

**Problem:** Three short fragments in a row reads like a Facebook ad. The "minutes" claim also sets up a promise that could feel like hype to someone who's been burned by "easy setup" promises before.

**Rewrite:**
> Real problems people had. These are the apps they built to fix them.

---

## HowItWorksSection

### Issue 9: "It just works. Forever." oversells
**Type:** Oversells

**Current:**
> It just works. Forever.

**Problem:** This is a promise nobody can make. "Forever" is the kind of word that makes burned people flinch. It sounds like the pitch they heard last time. Confident is good; guaranteeing eternity is not.

**Rewrite:**
> It runs. You're done.

Or:
> It just keeps running.

---

### Issue 10: "Start with a frustration, not a feature list" is brilliant but slightly lecture-y
**Type:** Mildly condescending

**Current:**
> Start with a frustration, not a feature list. "I waste 2 hours every Monday on this." That's enough. Meldar takes it from there.

**Problem:** "Not a feature list" assumes the reader knows what a feature list is or would naturally provide one. For truly non-technical users, this distinction doesn't exist. The example quote afterward ("I waste 2 hours every Monday on this") is perfect though.

**Rewrite:**
> Just say what bugs you. Something like: "I waste 2 hours every Monday on this." That's all Meldar needs.

---

### Issue 11: "Pick the design you like. Suggest changes. It's your thing." -- good but rushed
**Type:** Tone inconsistency (warm section, suddenly clipped)

**Current:**
> Meldar designs and builds your personal app. You see the progress in real time. Pick the design you like. Suggest changes. It's your thing.

**Problem:** The first two sentences are warm and visual. Then it snaps into three machine-gun fragments that feel like a different writer took over. The warmth drops.

**Rewrite:**
> Meldar designs and builds your personal app while you watch. You pick the look, suggest changes, make it yours.

---

## TiersSection

### Issue 12: "Activity pattern analysis" is pure jargon
**Type:** Jargon

**Current (Explorer features):**
> Activity pattern analysis

**Problem:** Nobody in the target audience would say these three words in a sentence. This reads like a product spec, not a benefit.

**Rewrite:**
> We show you where your time actually goes

---

### Issue 13: "Privacy-first -- metadata only" is jargon
**Type:** Jargon

**Current (Explorer features):**
> Privacy-first -- metadata only

**Problem:** "Metadata" is a word most non-technical people have never used. "Privacy-first" is a tech industry buzzword. Both fail the audience test.

**Rewrite:**
> We never see what you type or read

---

### Issue 14: "Curated automation library" is jargon
**Type:** Jargon

**Current (Builder features):**
> Curated automation library

**Problem:** Three jargon words in a row. "Curated" is a word from tech/media. "Automation" the audience barely understands. "Library" in this context is technical.

**Rewrite:**
> Ready-made apps you can use right away

---

### Issue 15: "Your own AI account" is vague and slightly technical
**Type:** Jargon / unclear

**Current (Builder features):**
> Your own AI account

**Problem:** What does this mean to someone who doesn't know what an AI account is? It sounds like you're signing them up for yet another thing.

**Rewrite:**
> Everything set up under your name

---

### Issue 16: "Full guided setup" is SaaS-speak
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current (Builder features):**
> Full guided setup

**Problem:** "Guided setup" is what every app says. It's invisible to someone who's seen it a hundred times.

**Rewrite:**
> We walk you through every step

---

### Issue 17: Explorer tier description has a tone slip
**Type:** Tone inconsistency

**Current:**
> Find out where your time actually goes. We watch your patterns and tell you what to automate.

**Problem:** "We watch your patterns" sounds slightly surveillance-y. Combined with an audience scared of AI, this phrasing could backfire.

**Rewrite:**
> Find out where your time actually goes. We'll spot the stuff worth automating.

---

### Issue 18: Studio tier description goes cold
**Type:** Tone inconsistency

**Current:**
> Tell us what you need. Our AI builds it while you watch. Design, code, everything.

**Problem:** "Design, code, everything" is a feature list fragment, not a warm sentence. It breaks from the conversational voice.

**Rewrite:**
> Tell us what you need. Our AI builds the whole thing while you watch -- the design, the logic, all of it.

---

## ComparisonSection

### Issue 19: "Starting point" / "Your frustration" feels forced
**Type:** Oversells

**Current:**
> Starting point: Your frustration (Meldar) vs. A blank prompt (Others)

**Problem:** Positioning "your frustration" as a positive is clever, but slightly tone-deaf. Nobody wants their frustration to be the selling point. It reads like the company is happy you're frustrated.

**Rewrite:**
> How it starts: You say what's annoying you (Meldar) vs. You stare at a blank screen (Others)

---

### Issue 20: "Harvested or locked in" is dramatic
**Type:** Oversells / fear-mongering

**Current:**
> Your data: Yours. Always. (Meldar) vs. Harvested or locked in (Others)

**Problem:** "Harvested" is a loaded word that feels like it's trying to scare the reader. For an audience already scared of AI, piling on more fear can feel manipulative rather than reassuring.

**Rewrite:**
> Your data: Yours. Always. (Meldar) vs. It depends on their terms (Others)

---

### Issue 21: Section heading is bland
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current:**
> How Meldar is different

**Problem:** Every comparison section on every SaaS site says "How [Product] is different." It's invisible.

**Rewrite:**
> Why people switch to Meldar

Or:
> Meldar vs. "figure it out yourself"

## TrustSection

### Issue 22: "European privacy law protects everything" is slightly clinical
**Type:** Tone inconsistency (warm section turns legalistic)

**Current:**
> We only see which apps you switch between and how often. Never what you type, read, or look at. No screen recordings. No passwords. No messages. European privacy law protects everything. You can delete it all, anytime.

**Problem:** Everything up to "No messages" is perfect -- direct, personal, reassuring. Then "European privacy law protects everything" suddenly reads like a compliance document. The warmth drops for one sentence.

**Rewrite:**
> ...No screen recordings. No passwords. No messages. It's all protected under European privacy law -- the strictest in the world. And you can delete everything, anytime.

---

### Issue 23: "Our privacy promise" link text is fine but corporate
**Type:** Sounds like a tech company

**Current:**
> Our privacy promise

**Rewrite:**
> Read exactly what we do (and don't) see

## FaqSection

### Issue 24: "convenience fee" sounds like Ticketmaster
**Type:** Wrong association

**Current:**
> We add a small convenience fee on top.

**Problem:** "Convenience fee" has extremely negative connotations for young people. It's what Ticketmaster charges. It's what the parking garage adds. It's universally hated.

**Rewrite:**
> We add a small service fee so we can keep the lights on.

Or:
> There's a small Meldar fee on top -- that's how we stay running.

---

### Issue 25: "Like a phone bill -- you pay for what you use" is outdated
**Type:** A 20-year-old would roll their eyes

**Current:**
> Like a phone bill -- you pay for what you use.

**Problem:** Most 18-28 year olds have had unlimited phone plans their entire lives. "Like a phone bill" means nothing to them, or worse, it means "confusing charges I don't understand."

**Rewrite:**
> The more you build, the more it costs. Most people spend $5-20 a month.

---

### Issue 26: FAQ answers are slightly long
**Type:** Sentences too long for the section

**Current (example):**
> Because those tools assume you already know what you're doing. Meldar starts from zero. It figures out what's worth automating in YOUR day, sets everything up for you, and stays with you until it works.

**Problem:** This is 3 sentences with a long compound final sentence. For an FAQ accordion, people scan. The last sentence could be split.

**Rewrite:**
> Because those tools assume you already know what you're doing. Meldar starts from zero. It figures out what's worth automating in YOUR life, sets it all up, and doesn't leave until it works.

---

## FinalCtaSection

### Issue 27: "Ready to build your own AI app?" assumes readiness
**Type:** Tone mismatch with audience

**Current:**
> Ready to build your own AI app?

**Problem:** The word "build" implies effort. The entire page just told them they don't have to build anything. Also, "ready" assumes they've been convinced -- but scared people aren't "ready," they're "curious." This is also a very common SaaS CTA pattern.

**Rewrite:**
> Curious? Let's make your first one together.

Or:
> Your turn. What do you want it to do?

---

### Issue 28: Repeated tagline feels cold at the close
**Type:** Tone inconsistency

**Current (closing line):**
> Your AI. Your app. Nobody else's.

**Problem:** Same issue as in the hero. At the very end of the page, after all that warmth, closing on a cool ownership statement feels like the brand talking, not a friend. The last thing someone reads should be warm.

**Rewrite:**
> This one's just for you.

Or:
> Built around your life. Not someone else's idea of it.

---

## Summary of Patterns

| Pattern | Count | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Jargon that slipped through | 5 | High -- breaks trust with non-technical audience |
| Sounds like a tech company | 7 | Medium -- makes copy invisible/generic |
| Oversells or hype | 4 | Medium -- triggers skepticism in burned audience |
| Tone inconsistency (warm to cold) | 4 | Medium -- breaks the emotional thread |
| Condescending or lecture-y | 1 | Low -- rare, which is good |
| Eye-roll for 20-year-old | 1 | Low -- the phone bill analogy |
| Sentences too long for section | 1 | Low -- FAQ answers could be tighter |

### Top 3 Priorities

1. **Fix the jargon in TiersSection.** This is where the audience decides to pay. Every feature bullet needs to pass the "would my non-technical friend understand this?" test. Right now, 3 of the 9 feature bullets fail.

2. **Rewrite the FinalCtaSection.** The last thing someone reads matters most. "Ready to build your own AI app?" contradicts the entire page's promise that THEY don't have to build anything. This is the biggest single tonal miss.

3. **Soften the tagline.** "Your AI. Your app. Nobody else's." appears twice (hero + close) and sets a cold, ownership-focused tone that doesn't match the warm, empathetic voice in the middle sections. Replace it with something that feels like a friend, not a billboard.
