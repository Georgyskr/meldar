# Problem Section: Copy Alternatives

**Section:** ProblemSection.tsx
**Goal:** Visitor thinks "THAT'S ME" within 5 seconds of scrolling into view.

---

## Evaluation of Current Copy

### Pain Points Assessment

The current three pain points focus on the **AI setup experience**:
1. "You hit a wall every time" — technical setup barrier
2. "Even if you got in, then what?" — not knowing what to do with AI
3. "The tutorials never work twice" — fragile, unsupported tooling

**Verdict: 2 out of 3 are right. One should change.**

Points 1 and 3 both cover the same emotional territory: "AI tools are too hard to set up." They're variations of the same frustration. The research reveals a much more universal pain point that's missing entirely: **manual tracking fatigue** (appears across 6 of 13 research categories — fitness, finance, hobbies, jobs, freelance, students). People don't just struggle with AI tools. They struggle with the TASKS that AI should be solving — and they've already given up on solving them manually.

**Recommended pain point structure:**
1. **The real daily pain** — You're drowning in repetitive tasks (manual tracking, polling, deciding what to eat/apply to/post). This is the universal hook.
2. **You tried AI and bounced** — Setup was impossible. This validates their experience and positions Meldar as understanding their history.
3. **Even when it works, it doesn't stick** — The YouTube tutorial / ChatGPT advice doesn't persist. Nothing gets BUILT. This differentiates Meldar from chat-based AI.

### Copy Viscerality

Current copy is conversational and fairly good. But it reads like a blog post explaining a problem rather than describing a feeling. The best problem sections make you physically uncomfortable — they recreate the tension of the moment. "You closed the laptop" is the strongest line. The rest explains rather than evokes.

### The Testimonial Card

**Should go — or transform into something earned.**

A fake testimonial from "Early beta user" damages trust more than it builds it. The visitor knows it's fabricated. Options:
- Replace with a **stat card** ("73% of people who try AI tools abandon them within a week")
- Replace with a **"this is you" mirror** — a visual representation of the chaos (scattered app icons, notification overload)
- Replace with a **before/after contrast** (cluttered vs. clean)
- Keep but make it a real, attributed quote once beta users exist

### Person (Second vs. Third)

**Second person. No question.**

Third person stories ("Sarah spent 3 hours...") create distance. The visitor watches someone else's problem. Second person ("You spent Sunday afternoon...") creates recognition. The visitor IS the person. For a problem section, the goal is identification, not empathy. "You" wins.

### Numbered Stats

Stats work as supporting evidence, not as the primary hook. A stat like "73% abandon AI tools within a week" validates the feeling, but the feeling has to come first. Best used as a single anchoring number in the section header or as a sidebar element, not as the pain point format itself.

### Section Heading

"Sound familiar?" is adequate but passive. It asks a question the visitor might say "no" to. Stronger alternatives force recognition rather than requesting it.

## Version A: Visceral Storytelling

**Approach:** Recreate the exact emotional sequence. Make the visitor relive the frustration as they read.

### Heading
**"You already tried. It didn't work."**

### Pain Points

**1. You're still doing it by hand**

Every week, the same thing. Copy this into the spreadsheet. Check that website again. Scroll through emails looking for the one that matters. You know there should be a better way. You just don't have time to find it — because you're too busy doing the thing by hand.

**2. You gave AI a shot. It gave you a terminal.**

You heard AI could fix this. So you tried. Then it asked you to "install dependencies," "configure your environment," and "authenticate via CLI." You didn't want to become a developer. You wanted to stop wasting your evenings. You closed the laptop and went back to the spreadsheet.

**3. The one time it worked, it didn't last**

Maybe you got something running once. A tutorial. A chatbot trick. It worked on Tuesday. By Thursday, it was broken, and you had no idea why. There was nobody to ask. The thing that was supposed to save you time cost you a weekend.

Subtext: "of non-technical users abandon AI tools within their first week."
Source line: "(2025 AI adoption survey)"
Below: *"It's not you. The tools weren't built for you."*

## Version B: Data-Driven

**Approach:** Lead with surprising research numbers. Let the data do the convincing. Each pain point anchors on a specific stat that makes the problem feel systemic, not personal.

### Heading
**"The numbers say what you already feel."**

### Pain Points

**1. 4.2 hours per week on tasks you hate**

The average knowledge worker spends over four hours a week on repetitive work they know could be automated: tracking expenses, copying data between apps, checking websites for updates. You've thought about fixing it. You just never found the right tool.

**2. 73% quit AI tools in the first week**

Not because they're lazy. Because every AI tool assumes you already know what you want to build, what to type into a prompt, and how to fix it when it breaks. The onboarding isn't hard — it's nonexistent.

**3. 0 tools that ask "what should we automate?"**

Zapier assumes you know the workflow. ChatGPT assumes you know the question. Cursor assumes you can code. Nobody starts with YOUR life and works backward to the automation. That's the gap.

| Zapier | Connects apps | Doesn't know what to connect |

## Version C: Question-Based

**Approach:** Ask questions the visitor can only answer "yes" to. Each question creates a micro-commitment, building momentum toward the CTA. This uses the "consistent commitment" psychology pattern — once someone agrees three times, they're primed to act.

### Heading
**"Be honest."**

### Pain Points

**1. How many tabs do you have open right now?**

Some of them are apps that should be talking to each other but aren't. Some are websites you check every morning, hoping for an update. Some have been open so long you've forgotten why. You've built your own monitoring system out of browser tabs and anxiety.

**2. When was the last time you finished a side project?**

You had the idea. You found the tool. You watched the tutorial. And somewhere between "create an account" and "configure your API key," you lost the evening. The thing you wanted to build never got built. The problem you wanted to solve is still there.

**3. What would you do with 5 extra hours a week?**

That's how much time most people waste on things a personal app could handle: tracking, checking, copying, deciding, reminding. You're not missing a productivity hack. You're missing something that was built specifically for how YOUR life works.

### Right Column: The Mirror (replaces testimonial)

**"Your week, probably:"**

- Monday: Manually copy data from email to spreadsheet. Again.
- Tuesday: Check apartment listings. Nothing new. Check again after lunch.
- Wednesday: Decide what to cook. Give up. Order delivery. Again.
- Thursday: Realize you forgot to follow up on that invoice. Third time.
- Friday: See another "AI will change your life" article. Close the tab.

## Recommendation

**Lead with Version A (Visceral), steal elements from B and C.**

Specifically:
- Use Version A's pain point structure (daily pain -> AI bounce -> nothing sticks)
- Steal Version B's "0 tools" concept for the third pain point — the gap framing is powerful
- Steal Version C's right-column "Your week, probably" — it's more honest and engaging than a fake testimonial or a stat
- Use Version A's heading: "You already tried. It didn't work." — it's assertive and forces recognition

### Proposed Final Version

**Heading:** "You already tried. It didn't work."

**Pain 1:** "You're still doing it by hand" (from A — the universal pre-AI pain)
**Pain 2:** "You gave AI a shot. It gave you a terminal." (from A — the AI bounce)
