# Forums & General: What Non-Technical People Wish AI Could Automate

Research across Quora, HackerNews, ProductHunt, and general forums/blogs on what everyday (non-technical) people wish AI could automate in their personal lives.

---

## Use Case 1: Meal Planning & Grocery Management

**Sources:** Quora, ProductHunt, blogs, MakeUseOf, ThoughtfulMisfit

People consistently describe meal planning as one of the most mentally draining recurring tasks. The cycle of deciding what to cook, checking what's in the pantry, building a grocery list, and ordering or shopping repeats weekly and feels endless. Parents especially report that "80% of their time is spent dealing with feeding the family through prep, cooking, eating, or cleaning up afterwards."

On ProductHunt, multiple AI meal planners have launched (Ollie, MealPlanner, AI Meal Planner) that generate personalized meal plans based on dietary needs, pantry contents, and budget -- indicating strong market demand. On Quora, HelloFresh and Blue Apron are cited as partial solutions, but users want something smarter that adapts to what's already in the fridge.

**What people want:** An AI that plans meals for the week based on dietary preferences and what you already have, auto-generates a grocery list, and places the order -- closing the full loop from "what's for dinner?" to groceries at the door.

**Key quote (Quora):** "Services like Blue Apron provide a pre-determined menu and pre-measured ingredients, which eliminates meal planning and grocery shopping while simplifying cooking."

## Use Case 2: Household Chore Scheduling & Delegation

**Sources:** MakeUseOf poll, Quora, HackerNews, ThoughtfulMisfit

A MakeUseOf reader poll found the top chores people want automated:
- Vacuuming/mopping: 31.1%
- Laundry: 24.5%
- Dishes: 24.3%
- Wiping/sanitizing surfaces: 14.4%

While robot vacuums partially address floor cleaning, the bigger pain point is the *cognitive overhead* of managing all household chores: remembering what needs doing, assigning tasks to family members, and tracking completion. On HackerNews, users describe building custom systems (Trello automation, Discord bots) just to manage chore rotation.

On ThoughtfulMisfit, an AI assistant that sets recurring reminders for home maintenance (gutters, HVAC, pest control) and rotates chore assignments among family members was rated as clearly worthwhile.

**What people want:** An AI household manager that tracks all recurring chores, auto-assigns them to family members on a fair rotation, sends reminders, and adapts when schedules change -- reducing the "invisible labor" of household management.

**Key quote (HackerNews):** One user built a "morning routine app with comically large task timers forcing completion of morning rituals -- makeup, breakfast, meditation -- with audio alerts preventing procrastination."

## Use Case 3: Appointment Scheduling & Life Admin

**Sources:** YourAutomatedLife, ThoughtfulMisfit, ProductHunt, Quora

Scheduling and rescheduling appointments (doctor, dentist, car service, vet, kids' activities) is a universal pain point. On Quora, users describe setting calendar reminders for annual physicals and dental checkups but still forgetting or procrastinating on actually booking them.

On ProductHunt, AI scheduling tools are trending: Cal.com, CalendarJet (AI voice/text booking), and BeforeSunset AI (automatic daily planning and time blocking). The "AI Chief of Staff" category on ProductHunt specifically targets life admin for busy people.

**What people want:** An AI that knows your recurring appointment schedule (annual physical in March, dentist every 6 months, car service every 10k miles), proactively books appointments at convenient times, handles rescheduling, and sends prep reminders (fasting before blood work, bringing insurance card).

**Key quote (YourAutomatedLife):** "AI assistants book, confirm, and reschedule appointments" -- but users want this to happen proactively, not just when asked.

## Use Case 4: Personal Finance & Bill Management

**Sources:** HackerNews, HuffPost, Quora, YourAutomatedLife

On HackerNews, multiple users describe building personal automation for finances: automatic monthly index fund purchases, banking transaction imports into spreadsheets, and household spending ledger bots. The common theme is that people want to "set and forget" their finances but still maintain visibility.

HuffPost lists auto-pay setup for recurring bills as one of the top stress-reducing automations. Quora users mention wanting AI to detect unused subscriptions and cancel them, track spending patterns, and alert on unusual charges.

**What people want:** An AI financial assistant that auto-pays bills, detects and cancels unused subscriptions, categorizes spending, alerts on anomalies, and makes smart savings/investment moves on autopilot -- essentially a personal CFO.

**Key quote (HackerNews):** "Monthly index fund buys prevent overspending concerns" -- one user automated recurring investments to remove the emotional decision from saving.

## Use Case 5: Smart Home Routines & Environment Management

**Sources:** HackerNews (both threads), IoTSecure, Quora, ThoughtfulMisfit

This was one of the most discussed topics on HackerNews, with dozens of users sharing elaborate home automation setups:
- Bedroom lights fading on gradually at sunrise
- A/C turning on before arriving home based on GPS
- Temperature monitoring for elderly relatives' homes
- Automatic garden watering based on moisture sensors
- Laundry cycle completion alerts via Sonos speakers
- Coffee maker brewing at a set time every morning

On Quora, users describe wanting "optimal lighting and climate control settings by analyzing daily routines and environmental factors" and security systems that "learn household patterns."

**What people want:** A unified smart home brain that learns your household's daily patterns and automatically manages lighting, climate, security, appliances, and energy usage -- without requiring technical setup or dozens of separate apps.

**Key quote (HackerNews):** "Bedroom lights fade on gradually at sunrise (6:30-7:00am), living room lights fade on 45 minutes before sunset, hallway motion sensors trigger night-light mode after 11pm."

## Use Case 6: Travel Planning & Booking

**Sources:** Medium, TechTimes, Built In, YourAutomatedLife

Travel planning is frequently cited as a task where people spend hours comparing flights, hotels, and activities across multiple sites. On YourAutomatedLife, AI travel planning is described as one of the top automation opportunities: "AI generates itineraries, compares flights, locates accommodations, and completes bookings."

Users want an AI that knows their preferences (window seat, no layovers over 3 hours, prefer boutique hotels) and can plan an entire trip from a simple prompt like "plan a 5-day trip to Portugal in October for under $3,000."

**What people want:** An end-to-end AI travel agent that takes a budget and preferences, builds a complete itinerary with flights, hotels, and activities, books everything, and provides real-time updates on delays or changes during the trip.

**Key quote (Built In):** "AI dynamically creates itineraries that reflect user preferences, current weather conditions, and local events, and during transit, provides real-time updates on delays, traffic, and cancellations."

## Use Case 7: Health & Fitness Tracking with Actionable Advice

**Sources:** HackerNews, TechTimes, Oxford University study, Quora

On HackerNews, users describe automating health data collection: smart scales syncing weight to spreadsheets with rolling averages, Apple Watch data flowing into personal dashboards, and habit-tracking systems that limit social media usage. The gap is between *collecting* health data and *acting on it*.

TechTimes and other sources note that "AI insights translate into personalized fitness and health recommendations, offering bespoke exercise plans and dietary advice tailored to current health metrics and goals." But users want more than generic advice -- they want context-aware nudges.

**What people want:** An AI health companion that ingests data from wearables, understands your health goals, and provides specific, timely recommendations -- like "you slept poorly last night, skip the HIIT workout and do yoga instead" or "you've been sedentary for 3 hours, take a 10-minute walk before your next meeting."

**Key quote (HackerNews):** "Weight automatically pulls from scale into spreadsheet with rolling weekly averages" -- people are hacking together what they wish existed as a seamless product.

## Use Case 8: Email & Communication Triage

**Sources:** HackerNews, HuffPost, Quora, YourAutomatedLife

Email overwhelm is a consistent theme. On HackerNews, users describe building systems to filter important emails and text them to their phone, while routing routine messages through automated rules. Others build RSS digest systems to consolidate information intake into a single daily email.

On Quora, email templates ("Canned Responses" in Gmail) are cited as a basic but valued automation. People want AI to go further: draft replies, flag urgent items, unsubscribe from junk, and summarize long threads.

**What people want:** An AI email assistant that triages the inbox by importance, drafts contextual replies for routine messages, summarizes long threads, auto-unsubscribes from unwanted lists, and surfaces only what truly needs human attention -- turning email from a time sink into a 5-minute daily check.

**Key quote (HackerNews):** "Important emails filtered and texted to phone; routine emails processed by rules" -- people build elaborate filtering systems because no product does this well enough.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

1. **The "last mile" problem:** Many partial solutions exist (robot vacuums, meal kits, smart thermostats) but people want end-to-end automation that closes the full loop without human intervention.

2. **Cognitive load reduction:** The #1 pain point isn't the physical task itself -- it's the mental overhead of remembering, deciding, and coordinating. People want AI to handle the *thinking* about household management, not just the execution.

3. **Proactive vs. reactive:** Users consistently want AI that acts *before being asked* -- booking the dentist appointment when it's due, reordering supplies before they run out, adjusting the thermostat before you get home.

4. **Integration fragmentation:** Many users (especially on HackerNews) cobble together 5-10 separate tools and services. The desire is for a single intelligent layer that connects everything.

5. **Non-technical accessibility:** The gap between what's *technically possible* (HN users building custom automations) and what's *accessible to non-technical people* is enormous. The biggest market opportunity is making these automations work without code.
