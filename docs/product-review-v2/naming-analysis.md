# Naming Analysis: Replacing "Time X-Ray"

## Part 1: Why "Time X-Ray" Fails

### The wrong mental models

"X-Ray" triggers three associations, none of them useful:

1. **Medical.** X-rays happen in hospitals. They're clinical, passive, and you get them when something is already wrong. That's the opposite of the vibe Meldar wants — proactive, personal, fun.
2. **Surveillance.** "X-Ray vision" means seeing through things without permission. For a product that keeps saying "your data stays private," naming the core feature after a surveillance metaphor is self-sabotage.
3. **Corporate jargon.** It sounds like something a McKinsey deck would call a time audit. "Let's run an X-Ray on Q3 productivity." A 22-year-old scrolling TikTok is not clicking that.

### Why Gen Z specifically won't click

- **Too abstract.** "Get your Time X-Ray" forces the user to build a mental model before they understand what they're getting. What is a time x-ray? Is it a tool? A report? A scan? A quiz? Compare to "See your screen time breakdown" — zero ambiguity.
- **Not shareable.** Nobody says "check out my time x-ray" to a friend. It doesn't pattern-match to anything in their world. Spotify Wrapped works because "wrapped" is a word people already use. "X-Ray" is not.
- **Sounds effortful.** X-rays require appointments, waiting rooms, and someone in a lab coat. The feature is a 30-second screenshot upload. The name oversells the complexity.
- **No verb energy.** "X-Ray" is a noun pretending to be an experience. It doesn't move. Compare: "Get your recap," "See your breakdown," "Check your score." These feel like doing something.

### The deeper problem

The name was chosen from inside the building. The team knows what it means because they built it. But the user landing on meldar.ai for the first time has none of that context. They see "Get your Time X-Ray" and the best-case response is confusion. Worst case, they assume it's some enterprise time-tracking tool and bounce.

## Part 3: Top 3 Picks

### Ranking criteria: Clarity > Shareability > Brand fit

---

### #1: Screen Recap

**Why it wins on clarity:** "Screen" tells you it's about your phone. "Recap" tells you it's a summary of something that already happened. Together: "a summary of your screen time." Zero guessing required.

**Why it wins on shareability:** "Recap" is native vocabulary for anyone under 30. YouTube recaps. Podcast recaps. Group chat recaps. "Just got my Screen Recap" sounds like something you'd actually text to a friend. It also borrows from the Spotify Wrapped pattern without being a copy — it's the same energy (look back at your usage) but with its own word.

**Why it works for Meldar's brand:** Meldar's voice is "a friend who's brilliant at this." A recap is what a friend gives you — not a diagnosis, not a report, not an audit. Just: here's what happened, now you decide what to do. It's warm without being soft. Direct without being aggressive. And it leaves room for the product to grow — when Meldar adds Google Takeout analysis, "your full recap" still works.

**Risk:** "Recap" is common. It doesn't have the uniqueness of a made-up word. But for a product that needs to communicate instantly to first-time visitors, common is a feature, not a bug.

---

### #2: Screen Score

**Why it's strong:** Gamification in two syllables. "What's your screen score?" is a question that creates instant curiosity and competition. It turns a passive report into something you want to beat — or at least compare. For Gen Z, who grew up with scores on everything (credit scores, Snapchat scores, BeReal streaks), this fits naturally.

**Why it's not #1:** It implies a single number. The feature actually delivers a full breakdown with app-by-app data, insights, and suggestions. Calling it a "score" might set wrong expectations. Users might expect a number from 1-100, and when they get a card with hours and bar charts, there's a mismatch. You'd need to add an actual score to make this name fully honest.

---

### #3: Screen Receipt

**Why it's strong:** The metaphor is perfect. Your phone charges you time every day, and nobody ever gives you the bill. Until now. "Here's your receipt" is punchy and slightly confrontational — exactly the right tone for making someone look at their 6+ hour screen time. Plus, the landing page already uses "Data Receipt" in the DataReceiptSection, so this unifies the language across the product.

**Why it's not #1:** "Receipt" is a slightly niche cultural reference. Older Gen Z and millennials get it. Younger Gen Z might not pattern-match as fast. It's also a more charged metaphor — "receipt" implies you owe something, which could feel guilt-trippy for a product that wants to be empowering, not shaming.

## Implementation Notes

1. **URL stays `/xray` for now.** Changing URLs breaks existing shared links and SEO. If a URL rename is needed later, do it with a 301 redirect from `/xray` to `/recap`.

2. **Internal code names don't need to change immediately.** `XRayCard`, `xrayResults`, `XRayResponse` — these are developer-facing. Renaming them is a refactor, not a branding task. Do it when convenient, not as part of this copy change.

3. **CLAUDE.md needs updating.** Every reference to "Time X-Ray" in the project documentation should become "Screen Recap." This includes the product description, discovery engine section, and landing page section descriptions.

4. **The share mechanics stay the same.** The card format, the OG image layout, the download — all work identically. Only the text on them changes.

5. **Analytics events stay `xray_*` for now.** Renaming analytics events mid-stream breaks historical data. Add a mapping layer later if needed.
